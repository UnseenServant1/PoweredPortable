How to Size a Portable Power Station for Home Backup

Updated March 2026 · 10 min read

Buying a portable power station for home backup is one of the smartest investments you can make for emergency preparedness. But the most common mistake people make is buying a unit that is either way too small for their needs or overkill for what they actually want to power. This guide walks you through exactly how to calculate your requirements so you buy the right size the first time.

Step 1: List What You Want to Power

Start by making a list of the devices and appliances you want to keep running during a power outage. Be realistic here. You probably do not need to run your entire house. Focus on the essentials that keep your household safe, comfortable, and connected.

Common home backup essentials include: a refrigerator (to prevent food spoilage), a Wi-Fi router and modem (to stay connected), phone and laptop chargers, LED lighting for a few rooms, a CPAP or other medical device, and possibly a space heater or fan depending on the season.

Step 2: Find Each Device's Wattage

Every electrical device has a wattage rating. You can usually find it on a label on the device itself, in the manual, or by searching the model number online. Here are typical wattages for common household items:

Step 3: Calculate Your Daily Watt-Hour Needs

For each device, multiply its wattage by the number of hours per day you plan to run it. Add up all the results to get your total daily watt-hour requirement.

Example scenario: A typical household wanting to keep the fridge running, maintain Wi-Fi, charge phones, and have lights for one full day during an outage would need roughly: 1,500 Wh (fridge) + 360 Wh (router) + 240 Wh (lights) + 60 Wh (phones) = approximately 2,160 Wh per day.

Step 4: Add a 15-20% Buffer

Power stations lose some energy through the inverter (converting DC battery power to AC wall power). A good rule of thumb is to add 15-20% to your calculated requirement to account for this inefficiency. So our 2,160 Wh example becomes roughly 2,500 Wh as a target capacity.

Step 5: Choose How Many Days of Backup You Want

If you only need power for a few hours during a brief outage, a smaller unit will work fine. If you want a full day or more of backup, you need a larger capacity. For most people, having enough power for 12-24 hours of essential use covers the vast majority of outage scenarios.

Important Tips for Home Backup

Charge before the storm: Keep your power station charged to at least 80% during storm season. Most modern units hold their charge for months with minimal drain.

Know your surge requirements: Some appliances like refrigerators and air conditioners draw a surge of power when they first start up, often 2-3 times their running wattage. Make sure your power station's peak output can handle these surges.

Consider solar for extended outages: If multi-day outages are a concern in your area, pairing your power station with solar panels means you can recharge during the day and have power through the night indefinitely.

Test before you need it: Do a practice run. Plug in your essential devices, run them for a few hours, and see how quickly your battery drains. This real-world test is far more accurate than calculations alone.
